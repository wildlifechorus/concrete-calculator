How accurate is this preview?

The simulator targets a realistic preview for decorative casting work. It is calibrated against published industry data so you can dial in a recipe with minimal test pours and pigment waste.

What is modeled

  • Pigment + cement blending uses Spectral.js, a Kubelka–Munk pigment-mixing library (the same physics model paint chemists use for color matching). This gives much more realistic midtones than naive RGB blending.
  • Saturation curve: 1 − exp(−0.50 · pct). Color develops rapidly through 4 %, plateaus by 6–7 %, and is essentially flat above 8 %. Matches Solomon Colors' published 7 % saturation point. Dosage guidance is cement-type aware per SC Pigments' colour chart: 2–3 % standard on white Portland, 4–5 % standard on grey Portland. Each pigment carries a calibrated tinting-strength factor (Pardo 4660 = 1.13×, Negro 55 = 1.52×), derived by fitting the KM model against the manufacturer's own swatch hex values at 2–5 % dosage — reducing average sRGB prediction error from ~80 to ~27 units.
  • Sand visibility in a 1:1 cement:sand mortar is set to 30 % for a smooth mold-cast surface. Sand particles are inert — pigment only colors the cement paste, then sand grains dilute the visible color.
  • Wet vs dry shift: fresh / wet concrete is rendered 32 % darker than fully cured. This matches the "hose it down to preview sealed color" guidance from Solomon Colors.

What is NOT modeled

  • Pigment-specific tinting strength. The four products are assumed to have equal tinting strength. Real iron oxides vary by ±20–40 % between brands and mesh sizes (mesh 500 is more vivid per gram than mesh 325).
  • Water-cement ratio (modeled as constant 0.44). Higher W/C produces lighter, more porous, slightly chalkier color.
  • Carbonation and aging. Cured concrete (4+ weeks) is somewhat lighter than freshly demolded; the "dry" swatch approximates 28-day cured.
  • Sealer effects. A film-forming sealer darkens roughly as much as the "wet" preview; matte water-based sealers barely change color.
  • Sand source. The model assumes a typical pale silica sand (RGB 210, 195, 162). Spanish marble sand, beach sand, or grog will shift the mortar color.
  • CSA cement. Grey CSA blended into white Portland shifts the color toward grey; not currently reflected when "White Portland" is selected (assume CSA off for the white preview).

Always pour a test slab

Even with a physically-based model, batch-to-batch variation in cement source, sand source, water hardness, ambient temperature, and curing humidity will produce ±1 shade variation. For high-stakes pieces, mix a 100–200 g test puck with your target recipe before committing to the full pour.
